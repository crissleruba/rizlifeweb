THE
HISTORICAL BACKGROUND OF 'EL FILIBUSTERISMO'

Through his cultural and educational experiences, Rizal demonstrated to the
Filipino people and the rest of the world what a nation deserves and how to
address inequity.

BACKGROUND

El Filibusterismo was written in the age of a rapidly fading Spanish empire, whose colonial rule was under threat from rising liberty not only in the Philippines but around the world. In the early nineteenth century, Spain's American colonies unanimously rejected Spanish rule, diminishing its hold on its remaining Caribbean and Pacific holdings.

It was first published in Ghent in 1891 and subsequently transcribed into English, German, French, Japanese, Tagalog, Ilonggo, and other languages. A nationalist novel by an author considered "the first Filipino," its significance as a social document from late-nineteenth-century Philippines is often emphasized. For many years, copies of the "Fili" were illegally brought into the Philippines after the Spanish authorities declared it illegal.


El Filibusterismo reveals the true state of Filipino society under its control of Spanish authority. The sequel addresses sociopolitical issues raised in the Noli, including the abuses and hypocrisy of members of the Spanish Catholic clergy, superstitions masquerading as religious faith, the need for educational reform, the mistreatment and corruption of officials in the government, and the fraudulent claims utilized by certain social-climbing Filipinos and Spaniards.


The 2nd novel was dedicated to the remembrance of Gom-Bur-Za (Don Mariano Gomez, 73; Don Jose Burgos, 35; Jacinto Zamora, 37 years old). The first printed edition of El Filibusterismo, written in Rizal's own handwriting, is presently housed in the Filipiniana Department of the Manila Bureau of Public Libraries. It is composed of 270 pages of long sheets of paper.
