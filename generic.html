THE
HISTORICAL BACKGROUND OF 'EL FILIBUSTERISMO'

Through his cultural and educational experiences, Rizal demonstrated to the
Filipino people and the rest of the world what a nation deserves and how to
address inequity.

BACKGROUND

El Filibusterismo was written in the age of a rapidly fading Spanish empire, whose colonial rule was under threat from rising liberty not only in the Philippines but around the world. In the early nineteenth century, Spain's American colonies unanimously rejected Spanish rule, diminishing its hold on its remaining Caribbean and Pacific holdings.

It was first published in Ghent in 1891 and subsequently transcribed into English, German, French, Japanese, Tagalog, Ilonggo, and other languages. A nationalist novel by an author considered "the first Filipino," its significance as a social document from late-nineteenth-century Philippines is often emphasized. For many years, copies of the "Fili" were illegally brought into the Philippines after the Spanish authorities declared it illegal.


El Filibusterismo reveals the true state of Filipino society under its control of Spanish authority. The sequel addresses sociopolitical issues raised in the Noli, including the abuses and hypocrisy of members of the Spanish Catholic clergy, superstitions masquerading as religious faith, the need for educational reform, the mistreatment and corruption of officials in the government, and the fraudulent claims utilized by certain social-climbing Filipinos and Spaniards.


The 2nd novel was dedicated to the remembrance of Gom-Bur-Za (Don Mariano Gomez, 73; Don Jose Burgos, 35; Jacinto Zamora, 37 years old). The first printed edition of El Filibusterismo, written in Rizal's own handwriting, is presently housed in the Filipiniana Department of the Manila Bureau of Public Libraries. It is composed of 270 pages of long sheets of paper.

Inspiration and Reasons for Writing the Novel

  • Gomburza: Father Gomez, Father Burgos, and Father Zamora. Rizal believed that the Spanish officials’ treatment and execution of the priests in the Gomburza case was unjust.
  • Rizal wanted to encourage Filipinos to realize and break away from the mistreatment in the hands of Spaniards.
  • The need for reform in the education system.
  • Issues in the Philippines such as corrupt officials.
  • Threat of the growing social status of the Spaniards.
  • The power of words, not fists, to inspire Filipinos to speak out and fight back.
  • Form and structure of the European novel of the time.
  • Novels that influenced Rizal: Uncle Tom’s Cabin by Harriet Beecher Stowe, Les Misérables by Victor Hugo, The Count of Monte Cristo by Alexandre Dumas, and The Prince by Niccolò Machiavelli.

The Summary of El Filibusterismo

El Filibusterismo takes place in the late nineteenth century, during the final years of Spanish sovereignty in the Philippines. It was written as a sequel to Rizal's first book, Noli Me Tangere, therefore many characters and plot threads are carried over. Noli Me Tangere's protagonist is Ibarra, a well-educated and ambitious young man who seeks progress in the Philippines. He wants to marry Maria Clara before he is accused of plotting against the government and her father, Captain Tiago, cancels the engagement. Padre Salvi, a Spanish friar, devised the conspiracy that led to Ibarra's arrest.

Simoun, now a dominant figure, has tight ties with the Spanish governor-general. He discreetly promotes instability and corruption in the hopes of encouraging Filipinos to rebel. He manipulates important individuals and incites unrest among the oppressed. While Simoun plots a revolution, young Filipino students led by Basilio, Isagani, and other scholars advocate for peaceful reforms, including the formation of a Spanish-language academy. However, the authorities stifle their activities, branding them as rebels. Basilio, now a medical student, first opposes Simoun's radical beliefs, hoping that knowledge and reform can still lead to growth and development.

At the wedding of Paulita Gómez and Juanito Pelaez, two members of the fraudulent Filipino elite, Simoun intends to set off a bomb inside a lamp. The goal of this explosion is to assassinate senior Spanish politicians and spark a wider uprising. Simoun's scheme falls apart when Isagani, who is in love with Paulita, finds out about the plot and tosses the lamp into the river before it explodes.
